There is a tendency in KYC analysis to treat complexity as a problem to be solved — something to work through, diagram, and then resolve into a clean conclusion. An ownership structure is complex; you map it; you find the UBO; you're done.

This framing misses something important: **complexity that cannot be explained by commercial logic is, itself, a risk indicator**.

## What commercial narrative means

Every legitimate business structure exists for a reason. Operating subsidiaries are created to separate liability or operate in different jurisdictions. Holding companies consolidate ownership or enable tax efficiency. Special purpose vehicles are used for financing arrangements. Trusts are established for asset protection or estate planning.

When an analyst asks "why does this structure exist?", a well-run business can answer the question. The answer might be complex — it might involve historical acquisitions, tax planning, or multi-jurisdictional licensing requirements — but it is an answer. It has commercial logic. It is coherent.

When no such explanation is available, or when the explanation offered doesn't withstand scrutiny, that is the signal.

## The gap in standard KYC

Standard KYC processes are designed to answer the question: *who owns this?* They are less well-designed to answer: *why is it structured this way?*

Ownership mapping outputs — even good ones — typically document the structure. They identify UBOs. They flag sanctioned parties. But they often stop short of the next analytical step: does this structure make sense?

This isn't because analysts aren't smart enough to ask the question. It's because the question is not well-operationalised. There is no field on the customer risk form for "commercial logic — present/absent/unclear." The QA function checks that the UBO is documented, not that the analyst assessed whether the structure is coherent.

## Operationalising the question

Making "does this structure make sense?" a formal analytical step requires three things:

**1. A prompt in the workflow.**
The analyst needs to be asked the question explicitly — at the point in the process where they have reviewed the structure and are forming their risk assessment. This is where a well-designed agent can add value: surfacing the question, prompting the analyst to document their conclusion, flagging cases where the structure contains patterns (circular ownership, nominee-heavy arrangements, jurisdiction hopping) that commonly lack commercial explanation.

**2. A documentation standard.**
The analyst's conclusion — "commercial logic present: multiple operating subsidiaries in EU jurisdictions, consistent with retail banking expansion strategy" or "commercial logic unclear: three-layer holding structure with no identified operating assets" — needs to be recorded in the file. Not as a boilerplate phrase, but as a substantive observation.

**3. Calibration for EDD.**
Structures where commercial logic is absent or unclear should, as a matter of policy, trigger EDD unless there is a documented reason not to. This isn't automatically adverse — there may be a legitimate explanation that wasn't captured during onboarding — but the absence of an explanation is a data point that warrants further enquiry.

## What agents can and cannot do

AI agents are well-suited to pattern recognition: identifying ownership structures that share characteristics with known high-risk configurations, flagging jurisdiction combinations that warrant additional scrutiny, and surfacing the question of commercial logic when it has not been addressed.

They are not suited to substituting for human judgement about whether a specific explanation is credible. That requires contextual reasoning — knowledge of industry norms, sector-specific operating models, and the plausibility of the explanation offered. This is precisely where human-in-the-loop is not a safety hedge; it is the core analytical step.

The agent can identify that a structure lacks an obvious explanation. The analyst decides whether the explanation provided is satisfactory.

## The regulatory framing

FATF Recommendation 10 and its UK implementation require enhanced due diligence for complex, unusually large, or unusual transaction patterns — and for structures with no apparent economic or visible lawful purpose. The principle applies equally to structures as to transactions.

Complexity without commercial narrative doesn't mean the customer is a money launderer. It means the customer has not yet provided a satisfactory explanation for how their business is organised. That is a compliance gap, and it warrants documentation and — in many cases — escalation.

Making this analytical step explicit, consistent, and documented is part of what separates a KYC programme that supports good decision-making from one that creates the appearance of due diligence without the substance.
