## Background

A large UK retail and commercial bank was carrying a substantial periodic review backlog — customers due for review under its risk-based approach who had not been reviewed within the required timeframe. The backlog had accumulated over 18 months, driven by a combination of operational capacity constraints and a period of organisational change.

The bank had launched an internal remediation programme but was experiencing three persistent challenges:

1. **Inconsistent quality**: QA sampling was showing high defect rates, with different analyst cohorts applying different standards to the same customer types.
2. **Slow throughput**: File completion rates were below target, partly because re-work was consuming a disproportionate share of capacity.
3. **Weak MI**: The programme management team did not have timely, reliable management information on progress, defect patterns, or analyst productivity.

## Our approach

We engaged on a sprint-based basis, embedded alongside the bank's existing remediation team and QA function.

**Sprint 1–2: Diagnosis and taxonomy design**

We began by reviewing a sample of completed files — both QA-passed and QA-failed — to understand the defect landscape. From this, we developed a structured defect taxonomy: 12 defect categories mapped to specific policy requirements, each with a severity grade (critical, significant, minor) and a documented root-cause category.

The taxonomy was agreed with the bank's compliance function and QA leads, then used as the basis for a revised QA scorecard.

**Sprint 3–4: Queue management and workflow structure**

We implemented a structured queue management approach that:

- Prioritised the backlog by risk tier (higher-risk customers moved to the front of the queue regardless of age)
- Pre-allocated files by customer type, ensuring analysts worked within their competency (e.g., complex corporate structures allocated to senior analysts)
- Introduced a pre-submission checklist — not a form-filling exercise, but a structured prompt that reduced "near-complete" files being submitted with gaps

**Sprint 5–6: QA cycle integration**

The revised QA scorecard was integrated into a weekly QA cycle:

- A 10% random sample was scored against the taxonomy each week
- Defect patterns were reviewed in a weekly QA calibration meeting with team leads
- Critical defects triggered immediate re-work and analyst feedback; systematic defect patterns triggered targeted refresher guidance
- Defect rates were tracked by analyst cohort, team, and customer type, enabling targeted support

**Sprint 7–8: MI and reporting**

A management information pack was developed for the programme management team, with weekly updates covering:

- File completion rate vs. target
- QA pass rate and trend
- Defect breakdown by category and severity
- Re-work volume and root cause
- Outstanding backlog by risk tier

## Outcomes

By the end of the engagement, the programme was observing:

- A measurable reduction in QA defect rates (illustrative: 40–50% reduction in re-work volume from the baseline; actual outcomes depend on starting position)
- Improved throughput driven by reduced re-work and more effective pre-submission quality checking
- Clear MI enabling programme managers to make informed resourcing and prioritisation decisions
- A QA scorecard and defect taxonomy the bank's QA function could continue to operate independently after engagement close

## What we would emphasise

The improvement in this programme was not primarily driven by technology. It was driven by:

1. **Explicit quality standards**: defects cannot be reduced if analysts don't know what a defect looks like
2. **Feedback loops**: QA results need to reach analysts quickly enough to change behaviour in the same programme
3. **Calibration**: QA teams need to agree on what they're measuring; uncalibrated QA produces noise, not signal

AI agent tooling — specifically an evidence checklist agent and a defect pattern identifier — supported the workflow. But the foundational work was design: making the quality standard explicit, the feedback loop tight, and the MI reliable.
